## 1. Purpose

This document establishes a disciplined way to record human contributions that influenced the development of HACS.

The goal is not to retroactively convert conversations into scientific evidence. The goal is to distinguish different forms of contribution clearly enough that attribution remains fair, traceable, and methodologically honest.

A contribution may influence the research without constituting empirical validation.

---

## 2. Contribution Classes

HACS uses the following contribution classes.

### A. Design Input
A suggestion, concern, or interaction that materially shaped how a concept, architecture, workflow, or research question was formulated.

### B. Practitioner Perspective
A perspective grounded in professional or practical experience that helps reveal constraints, risks, or requirements within a domain.

### C. Domain-Informed Observation
An observation informed by subject-matter knowledge that may motivate a hypothesis or design requirement, but is not by itself a controlled empirical result.

### D. Conceptual Challenge
A question, disagreement, reframing, or critique that caused a concept to be clarified, narrowed, expanded, or reformulated.

### E. Conceptual Contribution
A clearly identifiable idea or formulation that became part of the research framework.

### F. Empirical Evidence
Data or observations collected under a defined method that support or challenge a research hypothesis.

Only category F should be described as empirical evidence.

---

## 3. Attribution Rules

Before a named individual is described as a contributor in a public research artifact, the following should be verified where possible:

1. the underlying interaction or source exists;
2. the contribution can be described without exaggeration;
3. the contribution category is accurate;
4. direct quotations are not used without permission or an appropriate public source;
5. sensitive or private information is excluded;
6. authorship, acknowledgement, consultation, and research participation are not conflated;
7. attribution does not imply endorsement of the entire HACS framework.

Where source verification is incomplete, the record should remain marked as **pending verification** rather than filled from memory.

## 6. Why This Mapping Matters

HACS argues that durable collaboration requires provenance and accountability. The research process should therefore apply the same principle to itself.

If a human interaction influenced the framework, the project should preserve:

- who contributed,
- what was contributed,
- how the contribution was interpreted,
- what changed because of it,
- and what level of evidentiary weight the contribution actually carries.

This prevents two opposite errors:

1. erasing meaningful human influence from a human–AI research process;
2. overstating informal conversations as scientific validation.

> A domain participant raised [specific issue]. This was treated as a [contribution class], not empirical evidence. The interaction prompted HACS to clarify [specific concept or requirement].
