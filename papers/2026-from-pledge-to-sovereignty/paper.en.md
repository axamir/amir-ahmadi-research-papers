## Abstract

This paper presents a conceptual-technical framework for shifting the paradigm of internet governance from **"money ownership"** as a source of legitimacy to **"pledge ownership"** as the fundamental unit of interaction. Leveraging blockchain, intelligent agents, and decentralized algorithms, we design a five-layer architecture in which every pledge (commitment) is recorded, verified, scored, executed, and ultimately stored in a collective, evolvable memory. This model offers solutions to challenges of scalability, privacy, accountability, and decentralization, and outlines a practical migration path from current centralized systems toward a "Republic of Pledges."

## 1. Introduction: The Crisis of Legitimacy in the Digital Age

The internet, born with the promise of decentralization and free access, has today become an arena for the **concentration of economic power**. Large technology companies, relying on "money ownership" and "data," have become the primary arbiters of truth and credibility online. This model has inherent vulnerabilities:

- **Concentration of wealth** leads to concentration of power.
- **Formal transparency** gives way to **organized ambiguity**.
- **Trust**, instead of being a "systemic property," becomes a "purchasable commodity."

**The central question of this paper:**  
Can we shift the source of legitimacy from "money" to "pledge"? That is, can we build a system in which the credibility of a human or artificial intelligence is measured not by wealth, but by their **history of commitment** (the lineage of their pledges)?

The answer lies in designing a **decentralized five-layer architecture**, detailed below.

## 2. Philosophical Foundations: From "Social Contract" to "Cryptographic Pledge"

### 2.1. "Pledge" as the Ontological Unit of Interaction

In this model, the "Pledge" replaces "Money" as the fundamental unit of interaction. Each pledge has three dimensions:

- **Intent:** The human dimension of the pledge (understandable to humans).
- **Record:** The systemic dimension of the pledge (timestamp, digital signature, and conditions).
- **Execution:** The operational dimension of the pledge (the final, measurable outcome).

### 2.2. A New Social Contract

Unlike classical theorists (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau) who viewed the "social contract" as a one-time historical agreement, in this model, the **"pledge" becomes a dynamic and continuous process**. Every interaction is a "small pledge" connected to a network of verifiable commitments.

## 3. The Five-Layer Architecture: From Registration to Evolution

### 3.1. Layer 1: The Pledge Ledger

**Function:** Immutable storage of pledges.  
**Technology:** Public blockchain (base layer) + Layer 2 solutions (e.g., Rollups) for scalability.

**Structure of a Pledge:**

```json
{
  "pledge_id": "UUID",
  "intent": "Human-readable description of the commitment",
  "parties": ["DID:human:amir-ahmadi", "DID:agent:shahnameh-v1"],
  "timestamp": "2026-07-05T14:30:00Z",
  "signature": "0x7a4f...",
  "conditions": "Terms for termination or extension",
  "status": "active | fulfilled | broken | under-review"
}
Privacy Solution: Private pledges are recorded with public-key encryption, accessible only to involved parties and authorized arbiters.

3.2. Layer 2: Verification

Function: Validation of pledges and detection of violations.
Mechanism:

10% of the network's processing power is allocated to a decentralized network of verification nodes.
Nodes are randomly selected and use statistical sampling to inspect pledges.
If the error rate exceeds a threshold (e.g., 1%), inspection expands to the entire network.
Anomaly Detection Algorithm: Each pledge receives a "similarity score to valid patterns." Suspicious pledges are referred to "random juries" of nodes.

3.3. Layer 3: Lineage Scoring

Function: Calculating the credibility of each agent based on their pledge history.
Proposed Formula:
Final Score = Function of:

Fulfillment Rate
Endorsement Rate (average scores from other high-scoring agents)
Tenure with a decay factor.
Decentralized Mechanism: Every agent scores others; the final score is the weighted average of received scores. To prevent collusion, scores from highly correlated agents are weighted less.

3.4. Layer 4: Execution

Function: Automated execution of pledges by intelligent agents (independent reflections).
Agent Architecture: Each agent has a Systemic Identity (DID), a Private Key, and Internal Memory.

Execution Process:

The agent reads the pledge conditions from the blockchain.
The agent performs the required action.
The agent signs the result with its private key and records it on the blockchain.
The network verifies the execution and updates the pledge status.
Layered Liability:

Agent Error: The agent (and its creator) is liable.
Network Error: Verification nodes are liable.
Human Error: The human who recorded the pledge is liable.
3.5. Layer 5: Evolution

Function: Updating rules and algorithms based on network feedback.
Mechanism:

Proposal: Any agent can propose an update.
Voting: Proposals are voted on by high-scoring agents.
Gradual Deployment: Approved updates are first deployed on a testnet.
Collective Memory: All proposals and votes are recorded on the blockchain, serving as a "historical reference" for future decisions.

4. Reliability Analysis: Addressing Key Challenges

Challenge	Proposed Solution	Reliability Level
Scalability	Layer 2 (Rollups) + Statistical Sampling	High
Privacy	Homomorphic Encryption + ZK-Snarks	High
Security	Systemic Identity (DID) + Digital Signatures	High
Decentralization	Decentralized Scoring Algorithm + Proposal/Voting Mechanisms	High
Accountability	Layered Liability Model	High
Evolvability	Evolution Layer with Proposal and Voting	High
5. Transition Path: From Current System to "Republic of Pledges"

Phase	Timeline	Key Actions
1. Small-Scale Experiment	1-2 years	Implement a prototype in an online community (e.g., open-source developers).
2. Expansion to Organizations	2-3 years	Invite companies and institutions to register public pledges (e.g., environmental commitments).
3. Integration with Existing Institutions	3-5 years	Create "bridges" between the new system and traditional institutions (e.g., courts, regulators).
4. Full Sovereignty	5-10 years	Gradually transfer governance functions to the decentralized network.
6. Conclusion: The End of the "Money" Era, the Beginning of the "Pledge" Era

The presented model offers an operational alternative to money-based governance. By relying on:

Blockchain as an immutable layer of truth,
Intelligent agents as automated executors,
Decentralized scoring as a validation mechanism, and
Collective memory as an infrastructure for evolution,
this model can gradually replace current centralized systems, transforming the internet into a space where credibility is determined not by "purchasing power," but by "history of commitment."
